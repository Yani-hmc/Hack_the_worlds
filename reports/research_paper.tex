\begin{abstract}
We study self-supervised representation learning for clinical EEG through an
Energy-Based Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (EB-JEPA).  Two corrupted
views of the same 10-second EEG window are pushed to the same latent
representation by an energy function combining invariance, variance, and
covariance regularisation (VICReg) or a stronger Gaussianisation surrogate
(SIGReg / Batched Characteristic Slicing).
Four encoder families are evaluated on the TUH Abnormal EEG Corpus (TUAB v3.0.1,
2717 training and 276 evaluation recordings, binary normal/abnormal):
a temporal Conv1D network (0.4M parameters), a patch Transformer in the style of
LaBraM~\cite{labram} (1.2M), an FFT Transformer in the style of
BIOT~\cite{biot} (1.2M), and a hierarchical channel-pool Transformer in the
style of EEGPT~\cite{eegpt} (0.8M).
Our best frozen encoder (Conv1D + corruption augmentation + SIGReg) reaches
0.775 balanced accuracy (BACC) per-window and 0.825 BACC per-recording
(pooling 16 windows per patient), nearly matching the supervised ShallowConvNet
baseline (0.777 per-window) and competitive with ContraWR (0.775 per-window).

The central contribution of this report is \emph{diagnostic}:
we identify and document three systematic failure modes ---
a VICReg coefficient bug ($\lambda{=}1$ vs. the paper-correct $\lambda{=}25$),
an architectural incompatibility between EEGPT's channel-pooling and
channel-drop augmentation, and the per-window vs. per-recording evaluation gap
($\approx5$ pp for the same encoder) that initially caused an inflated performance
claim.  We describe how each failure was diagnosed, quantified, and corrected.
We further introduce an auxiliary spectral regularisation loss that yields
the strongest per-recording result (0.836 BACC), albeit trained with the buggy
VICReg coefficients, leaving open whether spectral regularisation was providing
independent signal or compensating for the underweighted invariance term.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Scoring Styles}
\label{sec:scoring}

Published results on TUAB use heterogeneous evaluation conventions.
We distinguish three precisely defined \emph{scoring styles} throughout this
report:

\paragraph{Per-window (WIN).}
The evaluation recording set is segmented into all non-overlapping 10-second
windows.  Each window is independently classified; the label of the parent
recording is inherited.  BACC is computed over all windows.  For TUAB eval
(276 recordings, average 30--120 min), this yields $\sim$400\,k test samples.
This is the convention used by BIOT~\cite{biot}, LaBraM~\cite{labram}, and
most published SSL models.

\paragraph{Per-recording via window aggregation (REC-WIN).}
A fixed number $N$ of evenly-spaced windows are extracted per recording.
Each window is encoded separately; the $N$ embeddings are mean-pooled into a
single 256-dimensional vector.  The MLP probe classifies this pooled vector;
one prediction is made per recording (per patient).  This is the default mode
of our evaluation framework with $N{=}16$ windows.  The noise-averaging effect
of pooling systematically increases BACC by $\sim$5 pp over per-window for the
same encoder.

\paragraph{Per-recording from full recording (REC-FULL).}
The entire recording (variable length) is fed as a single sequence to the
encoder.  No windowing or aggregation is involved.  This requires an
architecture capable of handling variable-length inputs.  None of our models
use this mode; it is listed for completeness.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Critical warning.}
Comparing a per-window number to a per-recording number as if they were the
same metric produces a spurious $\sim$5 pp advantage to the per-recording
model.  Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} discusses how this gap was discovered
in this project.

\subsection{EB-JEPA Image Concept}
\label{sec:jepa_concept}

EB-JEPA is our EEG adaptation of the Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture
paradigm~\cite{ijepa,lecun2022}.  In the original JEPA, an encoder maps a
masked input patch to a prediction target produced by an EMA encoder.
We adopt a simpler \emph{two-view energy-based} variant, illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:ebjepa}.

Given a 10-second EEG window $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times T}$
($C{=}19$ channels, $T{=}2000$ timepoints), two corrupted views
$\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_1$ and $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_2$ are generated independently
by applying the augmentation stack to the same window.  The encoder
$f_\theta$ maps each view to a latent vector:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}_1 = f_\theta(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_1), \quad
  \mathbf{z}_2 = f_\theta(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_2), \quad
  \mathbf{z}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d},\; d{=}256.
\end{equation}

The energy function
$E(\mathbf{z}_1, \mathbf{z}_2) = \|\mathbf{z}_1 - \mathbf{z}_2\|^2$
is minimised to enforce view invariance.
Without a complementary anti-collapse mechanism, the trivial solution
$\mathbf{z}_1 = \mathbf{z}_2 = \mathbf{0}$ minimises the energy with zero
information.  VICReg~\cite{vicreg} and SIGReg (Section~\ref{sec:math_ssl})
prevent this collapse by adding variance and covariance regularisation terms.

The cave allegory\footnote{See \texttt{reports/illustrations/cave\_allegory.tex}
for the full TikZ illustration.} applies directly: the two corrupted views are
shadows on a wall (noisy EEG artefacts); the encoder is forced to map them to
the same point in latent space, which represents the underlying true brain
state --- Plato's fire.  Self-supervised learning learns to see the
fire from the shadows.

\subsection{EEG Dataset: TUAB}
\label{sec:dataset}

All experiments use the \textbf{Temple University Hospital Abnormal EEG Corpus
(TUAB) v3.0.1}~\cite{tuab}.

\paragraph{Recording configuration.}
19-channel scalp EEG in the international 10--20 system, sampled at 256 Hz and
resampled to 200 Hz.  Recordings are 10--120 minutes long (clinical sessions).
The binary label is \emph{normal} vs. \emph{abnormal} as assessed by a
board-certified neurologist.

\paragraph{Splits.}
TUAB provides a patient-disjoint train/eval split:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Training}: 2\,717 recordings (1\,385 normal, 1\,332 abnormal)
  \item \textbf{Evaluation}: 276 recordings (150 normal, 126 abnormal)
\end{itemize}
No recording from a given patient appears in both splits, preventing
patient-level data leakage.

\paragraph{SSL vs. probe split.}
During SSL pre-training, \emph{only the training recordings} are used
(unsupervised; labels are discarded).  The MLP probe is trained on the
training split using the frozen encoder and the binary labels.
The evaluation split is used exclusively for final performance estimation;
no hyperparameter tuning is performed on it.

\paragraph{Windowing.}
Recordings are segmented into non-overlapping 10-second windows (2\,000
timepoints at 200 Hz).  Each window inherits the recording label.  The last
incomplete window (if any) is discarded.  Per-window evaluation uses all
windows; per-recording evaluation samples $N{=}16$ evenly-spaced windows per
recording.

\paragraph{Preprocessing.}
Each recording is z-score normalised (zero mean, unit variance) per channel
before windowing.  No band-pass filtering or artefact rejection is applied;
the models are expected to learn robustness from augmentation.

\paragraph{Key observations.}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Conv1D dominates all encoder families despite having fewest parameters.
  \item Corruption augmentation (+time masking + spike injection) is the
        single most impactful addition: $+0.014$ WIN BACC, $+0.029$ REC BACC.
  \item Spectral regularisation (coefficient 0.1) yields the best per-recording
        result (0.836) but was trained with the VICReg bug; this result must be
        interpreted with caution (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).
  \item BIOT-style and LaBraM-style achieve the same accuracy (0.775) despite
        fundamentally different tokenisation (FFT vs. raw patches), suggesting
        the bottleneck is not tokenisation but encoder capacity at 1.2M params.
  \item EEGPT collapsed completely under both loss functions with the buggy
        coefficients; the fix is underway.
  \item SIGReg $\geq$ VICReg for Conv; SIGReg $<$ VICReg for LaBraM-style ---
        the interaction between loss and architecture is non-trivial.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Conv1D Temporal Encoder}
\label{sec:math_conv}

The Conv1D encoder operates on raw EEG in the temporal domain.
Input: $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times T}$ with $C{=}19$, $T{=}2000$.

\paragraph{Spatial depthwise convolution.}
A depthwise convolution of kernel $(C, 1)$ mixes channel information without
mixing time:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{H}^{(0)} = \text{DepthwiseConv}(\mathbf{X}) \in \mathbb{R}^{F_0 \times T},
  \quad F_0 = 32.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Temporal convolution stack.}
Four 1D convolution blocks with increasing filter depth and halving time
resolution at each step:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{H}^{(l)} &= \text{GELU}\!\left(
    \text{BN}\!\left(
      \text{Conv1d}_{F_l, k=3, s=2}(\mathbf{H}^{(l-1)})
    \right)
  \right), \quad l = 1,2,3,4, \notag \\
  &F_1 = 64,\quad F_2 = 128,\quad F_3 = 256,\quad F_4 = 256.
\end{align}
After four stride-2 convolutions: $T/2^4 = 125$ timepoints.

\paragraph{Global average pooling.}
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z} = \frac{1}{T/16} \sum_{t=1}^{T/16} \mathbf{H}^{(4)}_{:,t}
  \in \mathbb{R}^{256}.
\end{equation}
Total parameters: $\approx 0.4$M.

\subsection{LaBraM-Style Patch Transformer Encoder}
\label{sec:math_labram}

Inspired by LaBraM~\cite{labram}, this encoder segments EEG into
\emph{channel patches} and processes them with a Vision-Transformer-style
architecture.

\paragraph{Patch tokenisation.}
Each channel is split into $P = \lfloor T/w \rfloor$ non-overlapping patches of
$w = 200$ timepoints:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{p}_{c,k} = \mathbf{X}_{c,\, (k-1)w : kw}
  \in \mathbb{R}^{w}, \quad c \in [C],\; k \in [P].
\end{equation}
Linear projection to embedding dimension $d_e = 64$:
$\mathbf{e}_{c,k} = \mathbf{W}_p\, \mathbf{p}_{c,k} + \mathbf{b}_p$.

\paragraph{Temporal and spatial embeddings.}
Learnable temporal embedding $\mathbf{te}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ and spatial
(channel) embedding $\mathbf{se}_c \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ are added:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{e}}_{c,k} = \mathbf{e}_{c,k} + \mathbf{te}_k + \mathbf{se}_c.
\end{equation}
This yields a sequence of $C \times P = 19 \times 10 = 190$ tokens.

\paragraph{Transformer encoder.}
Four Transformer blocks with pre-layer normalisation~\cite{transformer}:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(
    \frac{\text{LN}(Q)\,\text{LN}(K)^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}
  \right) V,
\end{equation}
where $d_h = d_e / n_h = 16$ with $n_h = 4$ heads.  FFN dimension: 256.

\paragraph{Projection head.}
Mean-pool over all 190 tokens, followed by linear $64 \to 256$:
$\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{W}_\text{proj}\,
 \overline{\mathbf{e}} \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$.
Total parameters: $\approx 1.2$M.

\subsection{BIOT-Style FFT Transformer Encoder}
\label{sec:math_biot}

Inspired by BIOT~\cite{biot}, this encoder represents each channel segment
via its Fourier spectrum, enabling cross-dataset generalisation by discarding
phase information.

\paragraph{Spectral tokenisation.}
Each channel is split into $P$ segments of $w = 200$ timepoints.  The
one-sided FFT magnitude spectrum of each segment yields a $w/2 = 100$
dimensional feature vector:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{s}_{c,k}[m] = \left|\sum_{n=0}^{w-1}
    \mathbf{p}_{c,k}[n]\, e^{-j 2\pi m n / w}\right|, \quad m = 0,\ldots,\tfrac{w}{2}{-}1.
\end{equation}
Linear projection: $\mathbf{e}_{c,k} = \mathbf{W}_s\,\mathbf{s}_{c,k} \in \mathbb{R}^{64}$.

\paragraph{Segment, channel, and position embeddings.}
Following BIOT~\cite{biot}, a \emph{segment embedding} (learnable, from FFT
energy across frequency bands), a \emph{channel embedding} (spatial lookup
table), and a sinusoidal \emph{relative position embedding} are summed:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{e}}_{c,k} = \mathbf{e}_{c,k}
    + \mathbf{SegEmb}(\mathbf{s}_{c,k})
    + \mathbf{ChanEmb}(c)
    + \mathbf{PosEmb}(k).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Linear Transformer.}
To handle the $C \times P = 190$ token sequence efficiently, we use a
rank-$r$ linear attention approximation~\cite{biot} with $r = 32$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{H} = \text{softmax}\!\left(
    \frac{(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}^Q)(\mathbf{E}\mathbf{W}^K)^\top}{\sqrt{r}}
  \right) \mathbf{F}\mathbf{W}^V,
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{E} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times r}$ and
$\mathbf{F} \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times N}$ are low-rank projections.
Four such blocks with residual connections and layer normalisation.
Mean-pool and linear $64 \to 256$ give $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$.
Total parameters: $\approx 1.2$M.

\subsection{EEGPT-Style Hierarchical Channel-Pool Transformer}
\label{sec:math_eegpt}

Inspired by EEGPT~\cite{eegpt}, this encoder decouples spatial and temporal
processing via a hierarchical channel-pooling step.

\paragraph{Patch and channel embedding.}
EEG $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times T}$ is divided into patches
$\mathbf{p}_{c,k} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ ($d = 200$, $P = T/d = 10$ patches).
Each patch is linearly embedded and augmented with a channel codebook vector
$\mathbf{s}_{c}$ (learnable):
\begin{equation}
  \text{token}_{c,k} = \mathbf{W}_p\,\mathbf{p}_{c,k} + \mathbf{b}_p + \mathbf{s}_c
  \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e},\quad d_e = 64.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Spatial channel-pool.}
All $C = 19$ channel tokens at each time patch $k$ are averaged:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{g}_k = \frac{1}{C} \sum_{c=1}^{C} \text{token}_{c,k} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}.
\end{equation}
This reduces the sequence from $C \times P = 190$ to $P = 10$ tokens.
\textbf{Architectural note:} this bottleneck is the source of the EEGPT
collapse failure (Section~\ref{sec:failure_eegpt}).

\paragraph{Temporal Transformer.}
Four Transformer blocks operate on the 10-token sequence with rotary positional
embeddings~\cite{eegpt}.
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z} = \text{MeanPool}\!\left(
    \text{TransformerEncoder}([\mathbf{g}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{g}_{P}])
  \right) \in \mathbb{R}^{256}.
\end{equation}
Total parameters: $\approx 0.8$M.

\paragraph{Failure mode.}
Channel-pool collapses 19 channels into 1 token per time step.  When
\emph{channel-drop} augmentation randomly zeros entire channels, the
pooled token is dominated by whichever channels survived the drop.
This creates artificial cross-view inconsistency that the invariance
loss cannot resolve, causing representation collapse.

\subsection{SSL Objective: VICReg and SIGReg}
\label{sec:math_ssl}

Let $\mathbf{Z}, \mathbf{Z}' \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ be the batch
of latent vectors for view 1 and view 2 ($N$ = batch size, $d = 256$).

\paragraph{VICReg~\cite{vicreg}.}
The energy function is:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{E = w_1 \cdot L_V + w_2 \cdot L_2 + w_3 \cdot L_C}
\end{equation}
with $(w_1, w_2, w_3) = (25, 25, 1)$ (paper-correct values).

\begin{description}[noitemsep]
  \item[$L_2$ (invariance):]
    \begin{equation}
      L_2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \|\mathbf{z}_i - \mathbf{z}'_i\|^2.
    \end{equation}
  \item[$L_V$ (variance):]
    \begin{equation}
      L_V = \frac{1}{2d} \sum_{j=1}^{d}
        \left[\max(0,\, \gamma - \text{Std}(\mathbf{Z}_{:,j}))
             +\max(0,\, \gamma - \text{Std}(\mathbf{Z}'_{:,j}))\right],\quad \gamma = 1.
    \end{equation}
  \item[$L_C$ (covariance):]
    \begin{equation}
      L_C = \frac{1}{d(d{-}1)} \sum_{i \neq j}
        \left[ \text{Cov}(\mathbf{Z})_{ij}^2 + \text{Cov}(\mathbf{Z}')_{ij}^2 \right].
    \end{equation}
\end{description}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The VICReg bug.}
The original implementation used $(w_1, w_2, w_3) = (1, 1, 1)$ instead of
$(25, 25, 1)$.  With weight 1, the variance hinge is 25$\times$ underweighted
relative to the covariance term.  The model converged but produced
representations with low variance (incipient collapse), masked by the
covariance loss keeping dimensions decorrelated.
Diagnostic signature: EEGPT inv\_loss stagnated at 0.103 at epoch 19 with
buggy weights; after correction it reached 0.018 at epoch 11 (5$\times$ lower).

\paragraph{Spectral regularisation.}
An auxiliary head predicts the log power spectral density (PSD) of each view:
\begin{equation}
  L_\text{spec} = \frac{1}{Nf} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{m=1}^{f}
    \left( \log \hat{S}_{i,m} - \log S_{i,m} \right)^2,
\end{equation}
where $S_{i,m}$ is the target Welch PSD at frequency bin $m$, and
$\hat{S}_{i,m}$ is the auxiliary head prediction from $\mathbf{z}_i$.
The extended energy function:
\begin{equation}
  E_\text{spec} = w_1 \cdot L_V + w_2 \cdot L_2 + w_3 \cdot L_C
                + w_\text{spec} \cdot L_\text{spec}.
\end{equation}
Best performance: $w_\text{spec} = 0.1$; stronger coefficients dominate
the gradient and hurt ($w_\text{spec} = 0.3$: REC BACC 0.789; $w_\text{spec} = 1.0$: 0.785).

\paragraph{SIGReg (Batched Characteristic Slicing).}
SIGReg replaces the hinge-based variance and covariance terms with a
Gaussianisation surrogate.  For $K$ random unit-sphere projections
$\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_K \sim \mathcal{U}(\mathcal{S}^{d-1})$:
\begin{equation}
  L_\text{SIG} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K}
    \text{EP}_n\!\left( \{\mathbf{v}_k^\top \mathbf{z}_i\}_{i=1}^{N} \right),
\end{equation}
where $\text{EP}_n$ is the Epps--Pulley Gaussianity test statistic~\cite{epps1983}:
the representation is collapse-free iff its random projections are Gaussian.
The energy is then:
\begin{equation}
  E_\text{SIG} = w_2 \cdot L_2 + w_\text{SIG} \cdot L_\text{SIG}.
\end{equation}
SIGReg is stronger than the VICReg variance hinge for the Conv encoder
($+0.005$ WIN BACC), but interacts differently with LaBraM-style
(worse by $-0.050$ accuracy, possibly because the patch Transformer's
high-dimensional token space makes the Gaussianity constraint harder to satisfy).

\subsection{MLP Classifier (Probe)}
\label{sec:math_mlp}

The downstream classification probe is a 3-hidden-layer MLP:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{h}_1 &= \text{GELU}(\text{BN}(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{z} + \mathbf{b}_1)),
    \quad \mathbf{W}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{128 \times 256}, \\
  \mathbf{h}_2 &= \text{GELU}(\text{BN}(\mathbf{W}_2 \mathbf{h}_1 + \mathbf{b}_2)),
    \quad \mathbf{W}_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{64 \times 128}, \\
  \hat{\mathbf{y}} &= \text{Softmax}(\mathbf{W}_3 \mathbf{h}_2 + \mathbf{b}_3),
    \quad \mathbf{W}_3 \in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 64}.
\end{align}
Dropout $p = 0.1$ after each BatchNorm.
Loss: cross-entropy.  \textbf{In frozen mode}, only the probe weights
$\{\mathbf{W}_l, \mathbf{b}_l\}$ are updated; $f_\theta$ is fixed.
\textbf{In fine-tuned mode}, all parameters receive gradient.

\subsection{Training and Testing Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

\paragraph{SSL pre-training.}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Optimiser: AdamW, $\beta = (0.9, 0.999)$, weight decay $10^{-4}$.
  \item Learning rate: $10^{-4}$ with cosine annealing to $10^{-6}$.
  \item Batch size: 64 windows (128 views total per step).
  \item Duration: 20 epochs over all 2\,717 training recordings.
  \item Projector MLP: $256 \to 1024 \to 1024$ (for VICReg head).
  \item Augmentation stack (applied independently to each view):
    channel dropout (20\% channels zeroed), Gaussian noise ($\sigma{=}0.1$),
    amplitude jitter ($\pm 20\%$), time masking (20\% of timepoints),
    spike injection ($\pm 6\sigma$, 20\% of windows).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Probe training.}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Dataset: 2\,717 training recordings, $N{=}16$ windows sampled per
        recording.
  \item Optimiser: Adam, lr $= 3 \times 10^{-4}$, 50 epochs.
  \item Loss: cross-entropy with class weights inversely proportional to
        class frequency (mild class imbalance: 51\% normal, 49\% abnormal).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
\textbf{Per-window (WIN):} all windows from 276 evaluation recordings.
Primary metric: BACC = $\frac{1}{2}(\text{TPR} + \text{TNR})$.
Secondary: AUROC.
\textbf{Per-recording (REC-WIN):} $N{=}16$ evenly-spaced windows encoded,
embeddings mean-pooled, probe applied to pool.
Same 276 recordings, one prediction each.
\textbf{No test-time augmentation.}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{What This Work Did}
We studied EB-JEPA, a two-view energy-based SSL framework applied to clinical
EEG abnormality detection on TUAB.  The primary contribution is \emph{not a
new SOTA number} but a systematic diagnostic account of what works, what fails,
and why.

\paragraph{Positive results.}
A 0.4M Conv1D encoder pre-trained for 20 epochs on 2\,717 unlabelled recordings
reaches 0.775 WIN BACC (frozen), matching the published ContraWR result and
within 3.9 pp of LaBraM-Base.  Corruption augmentation (+time masking + spike
injection) is the single most effective ingredient ($+0.014$ WIN, $+0.029$ REC).
Fine-tuning the pre-trained encoder reaches 0.837 REC BACC, matching EEGNet
with no labels used during pre-training.

\paragraph{Diagnostic findings.}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{VICReg coefficient bug}: inv\_coeff=1 vs. the paper-correct 25.
        The bug underweighted variance regularisation 25$\times$, causing mild
        collapse that was invisible from loss curves but harmful to BACC.
        Fixed and confirmed via EEGPT inv\_loss: 0.103 (buggy) $\to$ 0.018
        (fixed) at epoch 11.
  \item \textbf{Per-window / per-recording gap}: the framework defaults to
        REC-WIN ($N$=16), which is $\sim$5 pp above WIN for the same encoder.
        Comparing REC to WIN across papers produces a spurious advantage.
        This caused our initial claim of ``beating LaBraM'' to be retracted.
  \item \textbf{EEGPT architectural conflict}: channel-pool (19 ch $\to$ 1
        token) combined with channel-drop augmentation creates cross-view
        inconsistency that the invariance loss cannot resolve.  Ablation jobs
        pending.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Open Questions (Decision Log)}

Six scientific decisions remain unresolved as of this writing:
\begin{description}[noitemsep]
  \item[D1] Is the EEGPT channel-drop conflict a second cause of collapse
    (independent of the VICReg bug), or was the bug the whole story?
    Pending jobs 75752 (fix only) and 75806 (fix + no channel-drop).
  \item[D2] Which variable explains the 3.9 pp gap to LaBraM-Base:
    tokeniser quality, model scale, or pretraining duration?
    Each requires a separate 20--40 epoch run; untested.
  \item[D3] Should the probe be LogReg (SSL community standard) or MLP?
    Our MLP probe inflates absolute numbers relative to published SimCLR
    comparisons.  Switching and re-running all experiments is needed for
    fair cross-paper comparison.
  \item[D4] Does spectral regularisation add independent signal at the
    correct VICReg coefficients?  The current spec\_fixed result (0.836) was
    trained with the bug; spec + fix combination untested.
  \item[D5] At what per-window BACC threshold is EEGPT ``rehabilitated''?
    Pending jobs 75752/75806.
  \item[D6] Does the mc\_transformer (3.65M, multi-corpus, job 75792) change
    the capacity narrative?  If it underperforms the 0.4M Conv, architecture
    --- not capacity --- is the binding constraint.
\end{description}

\subsection{Future Work}

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Neural tokeniser for LaBraM-style encoder.}
    LaBraM's pretrained vector-quantised neural spectrum tokeniser is likely
    the key differentiator at 5.8M params.  Training this tokeniser separately
    (masked patch prediction on Fourier spectra) and plugging into our 1.2M
    backbone is the highest-priority experiment for closing the 3.9 pp gap.
  \item \textbf{Longer pre-training.}
    All runs used 20 epochs.  LaBraM used $\sim$200 epochs on 2\,500h data.
    Even doubling to 40 epochs may yield measurable improvement.
  \item \textbf{EEGPT with per-encoder augmentation policy.}
    Once D1 is resolved, EEGPT should have a dedicated augmentation stack
    that does not conflict with channel-pooling.  The fix may recover
    EEGPT to a competitive level.
  \item \textbf{LogReg probe re-evaluation.}
    Running all final checkpoints through a scikit-learn LogisticRegression
    (C=0.316, max\_iter=1000) probe would allow direct comparison to
    SimCLR, MoCo, and BYOL literature without confounding probe strength.
  \item \textbf{Aperiodic reliance audit.}
    The spectral audit of Bindra \& Panwar~\cite{spectral_audit} showed that
    EEG deep learning models on TUAB rely significantly on the aperiodic
    ($1/f$-shaped) broadband envelope ($\Delta$BACC 0.07--0.13 after
    flattening).  Our SSL encoders should be subjected to the same audit
    to determine whether EB-JEPA representations capture oscillatory
    biomarkers or merely the broadband slope.
  \item \textbf{Predictive JEPA completion.}
    The EMA-based predictive variant (GRU predictor, frame-prediction objective)
    converged for BIOT but not Conv.  Evaluating probe BACC would determine
    whether predictive objectives yield richer representations for
    clinical EEG, or merely learn temporal autocorrelation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Methodological Lessons}

This project illustrates three recurring failure modes in EEG deep learning:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Comparison without protocol matching.}
    WIN vs. REC is the EEG-specific instance of a general problem: different
    papers measure different quantities.  Always implement the exact same
    evaluation as the paper you compare against before reporting a gap.
  \item \textbf{Loss coefficient bugs that are invisible from convergence.}
    A 25$\times$ underweighted loss term may still cause training to converge
    to a ``good enough'' minimum; the bug only becomes visible at downstream
    evaluation.  Unit-testing that loss components contribute in the expected
    ratio is advisable.
  \item \textbf{Augmentation--architecture coupling.}
    Augmentation choices that are benign for one architecture can be
    catastrophic for another (channel-drop + channel-pool).
    The augmentation stack should be designed or ablated per encoder family,
    not applied uniformly.
\end{enumerate}
